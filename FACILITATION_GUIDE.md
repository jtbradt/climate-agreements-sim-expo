# Facilitation Guide: Climate Negotiations Simulation

## Session Overview

| | |
|---|---|
| **Total time** | 75 minutes |
| **Lecture** | 20--25 minutes |
| **Simulation** | 35--40 minutes (3 rounds) |
| **Debrief** | 10--15 minutes |
| **Students needed** | 12--60+ (6 teams, 2--10 per team) |
| **Materials** | Projector/screen, printed country briefing sheets (1 per team), one facilitator laptop |

**Tool URL:** https://jtbradt.github.io/climate-agreements-sim-expo/

---

## Before Class

### Room Setup
- Arrange seating so the 6 country delegations can huddle and strategize privately
- Prepare name cards or signs for each delegation: **United States**, **European Union**, **China**, **India**, **Sub-Saharan Africa**, **Oil States**
- Print one **Country Briefing Sheet** per team (see Appendix below) — do NOT distribute other countries' sheets
- Have the simulation tool open on a projected screen. Click **Reset All** to start at business-as-usual

### Team Assignment
Assign students to 6 roughly equal teams. For added realism, you can assign strategically:
- Students who enjoy debate → Oil States or US (harder negotiating positions)
- Students with economics background → China or EU (nuanced cost-benefit tradeoffs)
- Students interested in equity/justice → India or Sub-Saharan Africa (moral authority roles)

### Optional: Pre-Class Reading
Consider assigning a short reading on COP history or the Paris Agreement structure so students arrive with context.

## Information Structure

The simulation is designed to mirror the **asymmetric information** environment of real COP negotiations. Each delegation knows its own situation in detail but must rely on other countries' claims — which may be strategically incomplete or misleading.

### What Everyone Sees (Public Information)
- The **projected tool on the main screen**, which the instructor controls
- After each round, the instructor enters all offers and shows the collective outcome: temperature trajectory, emissions pathways, and (starting Round 2) the Financial Summary
- The **headline metrics** at the top of the tool: 2100 temperature, total emissions, global abatement cost

### What Each Team Knows (Private Information — via Briefing Sheets)
Each team receives a one-page briefing sheet with **only their own country's** data:
- Their current emissions and BAU trajectory
- Their GDP per capita and growth rate
- Their climate vulnerability (baseline temperature, Tconv factor, qualitative damage assessment)
- Their abatement cost parameters (qualitative: cheap, moderate, expensive)
- Suggested negotiating priorities and a "red line"

Teams do **not** receive:
- Other countries' emissions, GDP, damage exposure, or abatement costs
- The exact damage function formula or cost curve parameters
- The full parameter table

### Why This Matters
This asymmetry creates realistic negotiation dynamics:
- **India/SSA** know they face severe damages but must *convince* others of this — the US/EU can't see India's damage curve until results are shown
- **Oil States** can claim high transition costs without others being able to verify the magnitude
- **China** can argue it's "still developing" — others see its emissions are huge but don't know its exact abatement costs
- **US/EU** may not volunteer that they face minimal damages, but this becomes apparent when results are projected

The **reveal moments** after each round — when the instructor shows collective results on the projector — are where information asymmetries collapse and the real negotiation pressure builds.

## Lecture Portion (20--25 min)

Use this time to motivate the exercise. Suggested structure:

1. **The problem** (5 min) — Global emissions trajectory, temperature targets (1.5°C / 2°C), why collective action is hard
2. **History of negotiations** (8 min) — Kyoto Protocol, Copenhagen failure, Paris Agreement. Emphasize the recurring tensions: historical responsibility, development rights, free-riding
3. **The economics** (7 min) — Explain the key tradeoffs at a *general* level (do not reveal country-specific parameters — let students discover these through the exercise):
   - **Damages are unequal**: Hotter countries suffer more from warming. Some temperate countries may face minimal damages or even small benefits
   - **Abatement is costly**: Reducing emissions has real costs that differ across countries based on energy mix, infrastructure, and economic structure
   - **Free-riding is tempting**: Any single country's reductions barely move the global temperature needle, but everyone benefits from others' reductions
   - **Transfers can unlock deals**: Paying vulnerable countries to reduce emissions — or compensating them for damages — may be necessary to reach agreement
4. **Exercise instructions** (3 min) — Explain the round structure, distribute briefing sheets, emphasize that each team's sheet is confidential

## Simulation Rounds (35--40 min)

### How It Works

You (the instructor) operate the shared tool on the projector. Delegations negotiate among themselves and then submit their policy proposals to you. After each round, you enter all proposals into the tool so everyone can see the collective outcome.

Each delegation controls three variables:
- **Reduction Start Year** — When they begin cutting emissions (2025--2100)
- **Reduction Rate** — How fast they cut (%/year)
- **Net Transfer** — How much they pay (+) or receive (-) in climate finance ($B/year)

### Delegation Roles

Each team should designate:
- **Chief Negotiator** — speaks on behalf of the delegation in plenary sessions and bilateral meetings. This prevents too-many-voices chaos.
- **Analyst** — tracks the numbers: what has been offered, what it implies for their country, what counter-proposals to make.
- Remaining members serve as **advisors** — strategize during caucus time, prepare arguments, and pass notes to the chief negotiator.

For smaller teams (2--3 people), one person handles both negotiator and analyst roles.

### Offer Sheets

Give each delegation a simple paper slip (or index card) to submit each round:

> **Delegation:** _______________
> **Reduction Start Year:** _____ **Reduction Rate (%/yr):** _____
> **Net Transfer ($B/yr):** _____ (+ = paying, - = receiving)

Collecting these on paper rather than verbally keeps things organized and gives you a record of how positions evolved across rounds.

### Ground Rules (announce before Round 1)
1. Each round has a **strict time limit**. When time is called, submit your offer sheet — whatever is on it is your position.
2. Only **chief negotiators** may speak during plenary sessions. Advisors may pass notes but not address the room.
3. Transfers must balance — total payments in must equal total payments out. The tool shows a red warning if they don't. An agreement is not final until transfers balance.
4. There is no enforcement mechanism. Agreements are voluntary (just like real COPs).
5. The goal is to reach a **global agreement** — but each delegation should also advocate for their country's interests.
6. Your briefing sheet is **confidential**. You may share information from it strategically, but you are not required to be truthful.

---

### Round 1: Opening Plenary (10 min)

**Format:** Formal plenary session — structured speeches, no cross-talk.

**Objective:** Each delegation states its opening position and frames its priorities.

| Phase | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Caucus | 4 min | Teams privately review their briefing sheet, discuss strategy, fill out their first offer sheet |
| Plenary | 5 min | Each chief negotiator delivers a **45-second opening statement** and announces their offer. No rebuttals. You enter offers into the tool in real time. |
| Reveal | 1 min | Show the **Emissions & Temperature** tab only. Read the 2100 temperature aloud. Do NOT show Costs & Damages yet. |

**Opening statement guidance** (share with students): In 45 seconds, address:
1. Your country's position — what are you offering and why?
2. What you need from others to go further
3. One sentence framing your country's values (equity, growth, responsibility, etc.)

**Facilitator prompts after Round 1:**
- "At current offers, we're on track for [X]°C. The 2°C target requires significantly more ambition."
- "I'll note that the temperature outcome depends on *everyone's* actions. No single country can solve this alone."
- "In the next round, you'll have time for bilateral negotiations. Find allies, make deals."

**What typically happens:** Most delegations offer modest reductions. The US and EU may be reluctant. India and SSA push for transfers. Oil States resist or stall. Temperature stays well above 2°C.

---

### Round 2: Bilateral Negotiations (12--15 min)

**Format:** Structured bilateral meetings followed by a plenary check-in. This mirrors the "informal consultations" at real COPs where the actual deal-making happens.

**Objective:** Close gaps through direct negotiation between pairs of delegations.

| Phase | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Bilateral rotation | 8 min | Two structured bilateral meetings (4 min each — see rotation below). Chief negotiators from two delegations meet face-to-face; advisors may attend but only the chief negotiator speaks. |
| Open negotiation | 3 min | Free-form period: any delegation can approach any other. Side deals, coalition announcements, last-minute revisions. |
| Submit & reveal | 2 min | Collect revised offer sheets. Enter into tool. Show **all three tabs** — the damage/cost reveal. |

**Bilateral rotation:** Assign two rounds of bilateral meetings to ensure key pairings happen. Suggested pairings (adjust based on class dynamics):

| Meeting 1 (4 min) | Meeting 2 (4 min) |
|---|---|
| US ↔ China | US ↔ India |
| EU ↔ India | EU ↔ Oil States |
| SSA ↔ Oil States | China ↔ SSA |

This ensures every delegation meets at least two others, and the critical pairings (US-China, EU-India, SSA-Oil States) all occur. Delegations not in a bilateral can use the time for internal strategy.

**Bilateral meeting guidance** (share with students): In your 4-minute bilateral, try to:
1. Understand what the other side needs to move
2. Identify a specific trade: "We'll do X if you do Y"
3. You don't have to reach a deal — but you should leave knowing what a deal *could* look like

**Facilitator prompts after Round 2:**
- "We've moved from [X]°C to [Y]°C. Progress, but still [above/below] target."
- "Now look at the damage distribution. [Country] faces a [X]% GDP loss while [country] actually gains. Let that sink in."
- "The Financial Summary shows who's paying and who's benefiting. Is this deal fair? You have one round left."

**What typically happens:** The reveal of asymmetric damages is often a turning point. India and SSA delegations gain leverage. A coalition of willing parties may emerge. The hardest negotiations are typically US-China and anything involving Oil States.

---

### Round 3: Final Plenary (10 min)

**Format:** Open negotiation followed by a formal closing plenary — mirrors the final hours of a COP.

**Objective:** Reach a final agreement (or fail to — that's also a valid outcome).

| Phase | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Final negotiations | 6 min | Open format — any delegation can speak to any other. All tabs visible on the projector. Encourage urgency: "The COP presidency is asking for final offers in 6 minutes." |
| Closing plenary | 3 min | Each chief negotiator gets **20 seconds** for a final statement: accept the deal, reject, or make a last conditional offer. Collect final offer sheets. |
| Final reveal | 1 min | Enter final offers. Display results on the **Financial Summary** tab. |

**Raising the stakes:**
- At 4 minutes remaining: "The Secretary-General has issued a statement urging all parties to find common ground."
- At 2 minutes remaining: "Two minutes. If you cannot reach agreement, the world stays on the current trajectory."
- Consider appointing one delegation (often the EU) as "COP presidency" — they can propose compromise language, but other delegations must accept voluntarily.

**Announce the outcome:**
- If temperature ≤ 2°C: "Congratulations — this body has reached a historic agreement to limit warming to [X]°C."
- If temperature > 2°C but below BAU: "We have reduced warming from [BAU]°C to [X]°C — meaningful progress, but we have failed to meet the 2°C target."
- If little changed: "We have failed to reach a meaningful agreement. [X] countries will face severe consequences from inaction."
- In all cases, show the Financial Summary and read the damage figures for India and SSA aloud.

### Managing the Room

**If negotiations stall:**
- Inject a news event: "Breaking: a major hurricane has devastated [region]. Delegates from SSA are requesting an emergency session on loss and damage."
- Call an impromptu "ministerial" — the two most stubborn delegations must meet for 2 minutes with you mediating.

**If one team disengages:**
- Privately remind them of their briefing sheet priorities: "Your country faces X — is your current position serving your people?"
- In larger classes, consider assigning a "civil society observer" role to 1--2 students who can publicly comment on the proceedings (as NGOs do at real COPs).

**If it gets too noisy:**
- Use the plenary format strictly: only chief negotiators speak, one at a time.
- Bang a gavel (or just a book on the desk) to call order.

**Time signals:**
- Use the built-in countdown timer in the simulation tool (preset buttons for 1, 2, 4, 5, 8 min)
- Give warnings at 2 minutes and 30 seconds before each phase ends (the timer flashes yellow at 30s, red at 10s, and chimes when done)
- Be strict on time — real COPs have deadlines, and the pressure is pedagogically useful

## Debrief (10--15 min)

The debrief is where the deepest learning happens. Use the **"Show Detailed Breakdown"** button on the Financial Summary tab to reveal the full NPV analysis during this discussion.

### The Reveal: Avoided Damages (5 min)

Click **"Show Detailed Breakdown"** on the Financial Summary tab. This reveals a hidden table showing:
- **Avoided Damages ($B, NPV)** — the dollar value of damages prevented by the negotiated policies vs. business-as-usual, discounted at 3%
- **Net Benefit ($B, NPV)** — avoided damages minus abatement costs minus transfers

Walk through the numbers:
- "Let's look at what your agreement actually achieved in dollar terms."
- "India's avoided damages are [X] — compare that to their abatement costs of [Y]. Was the deal worth it for them?"
- "Now look at the global total. The collective benefit of action was [X]. The total cost was [Y]. The ratio is [X/Y to 1]."
- "Were there deals on the table that would have made *everyone* better off? If so, why didn't they happen?"

If no agreement was reached: show the BAU damage figures. "This is what inaction costs — and it falls disproportionately on the poorest countries."

### On Information and Strategy
- "What did you know about other countries' situations? What did you assume?"
- "Did anyone misrepresent their costs or vulnerability? Did it work?"
- "When the damage results were revealed in Round 2, did it change the dynamics? How?"
- "Did anyone try to free-ride? How did others respond?"

### On Equity
- "Look at the damage column — India and SSA face the worst damages but emit the least. How did that shape negotiations?"
- "The EU actually *benefits* slightly from moderate warming in this model. Did that affect their willingness to act? Should it?"
- "Were transfers used to address equity, or just to buy votes?"

### On Real-World Parallels
- "What in this exercise mirrors real COP negotiations?"
- "In real life, what information do countries have about each other? Is it better or worse than what you had?"
- "What's missing from this model that matters in reality?" (Good answers: domestic politics, technology uncertainty, adaptation, loss & damage, NDC ratchet mechanisms, trust/verification)
- "Why is the Paris Agreement structured around voluntary pledges rather than binding targets? Did this exercise help you understand why?"

### On the Model and Discounting
- "The Burke damage function assumes climate affects GDP *growth*, not just GDP levels. What does that mean for long-run damages?"
- "We used a 3% discount rate to compute present values. What happens if you care more about future generations (lower rate)? What if you discount the future more heavily?"
- "We assumed constant growth rates. How would slowing growth in China or accelerating growth in SSA change the dynamics?"

## Variations

### Shorter Version (15--20 min simulation)
Run only 2 rounds: Opening Positions (5 min) + Final Negotiation (10 min). Show all tabs from the start.

### Longer Version (Full class period)
Add a **Round 0** where delegations research their country's real-world position and present a 2-minute opening statement before offers begin. Add a 4th round focused purely on transfer negotiations.

### Competitive Scoring
Assign a "national welfare score" to each delegation: some weighted combination of avoided damages + avoided abatement costs + net transfers received. Teams try to maximize their own score while reaching a collective temperature target. This sharpens the free-rider incentive.

### Full Information Variant
Give all teams access to the tool on their own devices. This changes the exercise from a negotiation-under-uncertainty game to a pure bargaining game. Still valuable, but different dynamics — teams can pre-compute optimal strategies.

## Troubleshooting

| Issue | Solution |
|---|---|
| Students don't engage | Add competitive scoring; strengthen red lines on briefing sheets |
| One team dominates | Enforce time limits on speeches; require bilateral meetings |
| Agreement reached too easily | Tighten the temperature target to 1.5°C; add harder red line constraints |
| No agreement possible | That's a valid outcome — use it in debrief to discuss why real COPs often fail |
| Tool not loading | Chart.js loads from CDN; ensure classroom WiFi allows it. Fallback: download the HTML file and open locally |
| Transfers don't balance | The tool shows a red warning. Remind students this must be resolved before an agreement is final |
| Students share briefing sheets | Remind them it's confidential, but if it happens, discuss in debrief how transparency changes dynamics |
